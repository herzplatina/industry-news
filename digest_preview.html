Guest post: AI Inference Is Breaking Unit Economics
Date: Thu, 7 May 2026 22:04:02 +0000

AI Inference Cost: The Hidden Unit Economics Crisis

AI products are facing a fundamental economic challenge that threatens their scalability and profitability. While software companies typically enjoy gross margins of 80–90%, AI companies are operating at significantly lower margins of 50–60%, with some fast-growing startups struggling with margins as low as 25% and operating at losses as they scale. This disparity exists because AI products scale like traditional software in terms of user growth, but their infrastructure costs scale proportionally with usage, mirroring the economics of hardware and data centers rather than software.

The mathematics of this problem become stark at scale. Consider a product serving 10 million daily requests at just two cents per inference. That generates over $70 million in annual revenue. However, a mere 30% reduction in cost per request would save over $20 million annually. At such scales, efficiency improvements in inference cost often matter more than shipping new features, fundamentally changing how company leadership approaches product development and engineering priorities.

The broader industry context makes this challenge even more critical. McKinsey estimates that global data center investment will reach $6.7 trillion by 2030, with $5.2 trillion of that tied specifically to AI workloads. Meanwhile, Gartner projects that global AI spending will hit $2.5 trillion by 2026. These figures underscore how the compute cost problem is becoming one of the defining economic forces shaping the AI industry.

The Dual Cost Pressure

The situation is further complicated by opposing forces pushing the cost equation in different directions simultaneously. On one hand, per-token costs continue falling as competition increases and technology improves. On the other hand, new reasoning models are pushing per-query compute up by an order of magnitude or more. A single response from a deep-reasoning system can consume as many tokens as what previously required a hundred separate turns. For companies building AI products, this means the cost curve is being stretched from above by demand for more capable reasoning while being compressed from below by efficiency gains. Which force dominates depends entirely on the specific workload. For a product answering simple queries, optimization wins. For one where users expect sophisticated reasoning before receiving an answer, a single feature launch can erase an entire year of efficiency gains.

This dynamic reduces the business model question to something deceptively simple: how much does each inference cost, and how can you reduce it? The answer to this question determines whether a product can scale sustainably.

Industry Leaders' Optimization Strategies

Companies with hundreds of millions of users have recognized that inefficient inference becomes a multibillion-dollar problem, spurring them to invest heavily in making existing models dramatically cheaper rather than simply building new ones.

OpenAI has achieved remarkable improvements, reducing response costs by more than 1,000 times over a 14-month period into early 2026. These gains came from full-stack optimizations, including automatic prompt caching that cuts repeated-query costs by up to 90%, specialized chips like Cerebras, and database-level optimizations.

Anthropic has similarly invested in prompt caching technology, noting that it can reduce costs by up to 90% while simultaneously cutting latency by up to 85% for long prompts, making it one of the most impactful techniques available.

Google has pioneered techniques specifically for search-like workloads, including smart request routing and staged inference. In its GKE Inference Gateway, requests that share a system prompt are routed to instances with a warm KV cache, cutting time-to-first-token by up to 96% during peak usage. The company also splits prefill and decode operations across different server pools, boosting throughput by approximately 60%.

Yandex took a different approach by stacking multiple techniques together. In a technical report on its Alice AI LLM base model, the company demonstrated a 5.8 times speedup by combining quantization, EAGLE3 speculative decoding, KV cache reuse for shared prefixes, and optimized parallelization. This combination reduced token generation time from 140 milliseconds to 67 milliseconds, showing the power of integrated optimization strategies.

Common Optimization Techniques

Across the industry, a standard set of optimization strategies has emerged. Prompt caching reduces both the repeated context cost and latency, making it particularly valuable for products handling long prompts or repeated system instructions. Quantization lowers precision (for example, converting from 16-bit to 8-bit or 4-bit) to shrink models, reduce memory use, and speed up inference while maintaining acceptable quality. Speculative decoding reduces token generation time, making it best suited for high-volume generation workloads. Smart routing prevents overuse of large models by directing simple queries to smaller models, which works well for products receiving mixed query difficulty. KV cache reuse reduces repeated prefix computation, benefiting shared prompts, agents, and enterprise workflows.

These techniques represent a maturation of the AI infrastructure industry. Rather than relying on Moore's Law or waiting for the next generation of chips, companies are achieving substantial cost reductions through sophisticated software and algorithmic improvements. The emergence of these standardized approaches suggests that inference optimization is becoming table stakes for any company planning to scale an AI product profitably.

The fundamental insight is that inference cost has become a defining business metric for AI companies, as important as unit economics and customer acquisition cost in traditional software. Companies that master inference optimization can achieve sustainable, profitable scaling. Those that ignore it will face margin collapse despite strong topline growth.

What It Means to Be Asian American
Date: Thu, 7 May 2026 17:58:45 +0000

Summary: What It Means to Be Asian American

Deb Liu's newsletter explores her personal journey of navigating identity as an Asian American and reflects on what this experience means for immigrant families and future generations.

Liu grew up in a small Southern town where she was visibly different from her peers. Walking down the street with her family, strangers would yell at them to "go back where you came from." The cultural differences that defined her household—the steamed buns her mother made from scratch, her parents' heavy accents, the shape of her eyes—all felt magnified and marked her as an outsider. Her classmates questioned why she would eat "something so weird" like the bao she brought to school. Facing constant reminders that she didn't belong, Liu's response was to try to become invisible. She attempted to be smaller, quieter, and less noticeable, hoping that if no one paid attention to her, the commentary about her differences would stop. This strategy failed. Week after week, jokes, slurs, and casual remarks reminded her that she was the "other." Some people meant these comments maliciously, while others treated them as harmless humor, but either way, they compelled Liu to shrink further into herself.

When Liu told her parents about these experiences, they offered consistent advice: ignore it and recognize that the people making these comments were simply ignorant. Her parents understood something crucial that Liu had not yet fully grasped—that survival often meant not making waves. When the local Southern Baptist Church rejected their family as members, they didn't fight or protest. Instead, they simply left and became Presbyterian, a change they maintained for the rest of their lives. In their new church community, they found connection and belonging. Liu's parents never allowed such rejections to visibly wound them, moving forward without much fuss despite the pain these rejections surely caused.

Liu's parents had immigrated from Hong Kong to America for college with almost nothing to their names. Through grit, sacrifice, and stubborn optimism, they built a life in their adopted country. They maintained Chinese language and traditions in their home. Rather than isolating themselves, they actively created community wherever they could find it, helping to establish Chinese school programs, Bible study groups, and friendships with other Chinese families scattered throughout the area. They also became part of the Chinese restaurant community. Despite living in a place that rarely reflected their own identity back to them, Liu's parents remained unapologetically themselves. As a child, Liu couldn't understand their confidence or why they believed living in this small Southern town represented their American Dream. She desperately wanted to blend in and be like everyone else around her.

Every few years, after months of saving money, Liu's family would travel back to Hong Kong. For a child accustomed to a town of fewer than 14,000 people, arriving in a city of six million on an island felt like stepping onto another planet entirely. The skyscrapers, billboards, and endless lights created a sensory overload, yet she discovered she didn't fully belong there either. Her relatives criticized how she dressed, noting she was too American. They commented on her appearance, saying she was too tan, too tall, and too fat. Shopkeepers who saw the family thought they didn't understand Chinese and insulted Liu and her sister in Cantonese, not realizing they could comprehend the language. The Cantonese her parents had taught her sounded old-fashioned and frozen in time, an older version of the language that had continued to evolve in Hong Kong without her parents' input. Her parents had inadvertently passed down what Liu describes as a linguistic time capsule.

This created a painful paradox: in South Carolina, she was too Asian, while in Asia, she was too American. For many years, Liu interpreted this tension as evidence that something was fundamentally wrong with her. Her perspective has since evolved into a more nuanced understanding.

Being Asian American, Liu has come to recognize, often involves living between two worlds and seeking belonging wherever it can be found. Asian Americans learn to translate culture, expectations, and identity. They develop code-switching skills—the ability to adjust behavior and communication based on context. They spend significant time lost in translation, navigating misunderstandings and cultural gaps. But through this experience, they also develop resilience.

Liu was struck by comedian Aziz Ansari's account of growing up in a small town in South Carolina, where his family, like hers, found community despite their significant differences from the broader population. This resonated deeply because many immigrant families essentially created islands of belonging in places where they otherwise felt profoundly alone. Liu's daughter Bethany conducted an interview with her grandmother before her passing, during which Liu's mother-in-law expressed gratitude for America and the life she had been able to build there. This conversation profoundly affected Liu.

Liu's parents and in-laws all came to America for college and spent the next sixty years building lives in their adopted country. From certain perspectives, they remained forever foreigners—their accents never fully disappeared, their traditions remained distinctly different. Yet they loved America fiercely and devotedly. They believed in the country even when America did not always fully embrace them in return. As a child, Liu thought strength meant blending in and becoming invisible. Today, she understands strength as something entirely different—the ability to maintain your identity and dignity despite external pressure to conform or disappear.

The experience differs dramatically for Liu's own children. They don't contemplate what it means to be Asian American because their experience is fundamentally different from Liu's childhood. Although they've attended Chinese classes since they were toddlers, they resist speaking the language unless required. They enjoy Asian food, but they live in a place where such cuisine is abundant and accessible rather than strange and marked as different. The newsletter ends mid-thought as Liu begins describing an event one of her children attended, suggesting the discussion of how the next generation experiences Asian American identity continues beyond this excerpt.

The Pulse: AI load breaks GitHub – why not other vendors?
Date: Thu, 7 May 2026 17:34:44 +0000

Summary: GitHub's Reliability Crisis and Industry Questions

GitHub has experienced a severe and ongoing reliability crisis that has drawn widespread criticism from both companies and prominent open-source developers. This newsletter examines the recent cascade of failures and what they reveal about GitHub's operational state compared to competing platforms.

The Alarming Reliability Statistics

The reliability numbers paint a stark picture of GitHub's degradation. Last month, third-party measurements recorded GitHub at one nine, meaning approximately 90 percent uptime. This month, the situation deteriorated dramatically to zero nines, with reliability dropping to just 86 percent according to a third-party tracker. These metrics come from independent monitoring services, suggesting the problems are real and measurable rather than anecdotal complaints.

The Data Integrity Catastrophe

On April 23rd, GitHub experienced what the newsletter characterizes as "frankly embarrassing": a data integrity incident involving pull requests merged through the merge queue using the squash merge method. When merge groups contained more than one pull request, the system produced incorrect merge commits. The specific technical problem involved commits being reverted from subsequent merges, effectively causing commits to be lost in the merged code.

This incident violated one of GitHub's fundamental integrity promises: that pull requests would merge as expected when using squash merge, a standard technique for consolidating multiple small commits into a single meaningful commit. A total of 2,092 pull requests across multiple companies were impacted by this bug, including prominent companies like Modal and Zipline. The problem forced affected customers to manually untangle their git histories and recover lost commits, work that GitHub was unable to assist with directly. Customers had to follow manual recovery procedures, reverting the squash commit and re-applying commits individually. Eventually, GitHub emailed affected customers with lists of impacted commits, but the response felt inadequate to those affected.

Can Duruk, a software engineer at Modal, expressed particular frustration with how GitHub's leadership handled the communication around this failure. He felt that GitHub's Chief Operating Officer was being dishonest by inflating denominators to minimize the apparent impact of the outage rather than offering sincere apologies that acknowledged how the incident fundamentally invalidated GitHub's core promise to customers. Duruk noted that Modal's team had to dig into GitHub's status page to even realize the severity of what had happened.

The Cascade of Ongoing Outages

The problems did not stop with the data integrity incident. On April 27th, pull requests and issues disappeared from GitHub's web interface. While the data itself didn't vanish, users couldn't access it for six hours due to an Elasticsearch outage on GitHub's backend. The cluster became overloaded and went down, rendering critical features inaccessible.

The outages continued throughout the week with additional incidents on April 28th, including some pull requests not showing up and problems with GitHub Actions. On April 29th, users reported incomplete pull requests in repositories. Additionally, on April 28th, security firm Wiz disclosed a critical security vulnerability where bad actors could gain access to all repositories on GitHub and GitHub Enterprise Server using only a git push command. While GitHub fixed the issue on GitHub.com within six hours, unpatched GitHub Enterprise servers remained vulnerable.

A Notable Developer's Departure

The crisis reached a symbolic peak when Mitchell Hashimoto, founder of HashiCorp and creator of Ghostty, announced he was leaving GitHub. His decision came after eighteen years on the platform and was driven by a simple but powerful observation: GitHub had made him unproductive. Mitchell maintained a journal during the preceding month, marking each day when a GitHub outage negatively impacted his ability to work. He reported that almost every day had an X marked against it.

On the day he wrote his departure announcement, Mitchell had been unable to conduct pull request reviews for approximately two hours due to a GitHub Actions outage. He expressed his frustration plainly, stating that GitHub was no longer a place for serious work if it blocked users for hours every day. His message conveyed both sadness and resignation, noting that while he wanted GitHub to be better and wanted to work there, the platform consistently prevented him from being productive, reviewing code, and shipping software.

Mitchell's experience suggests a critical gap between GitHub's official status page, which may present a rosier picture, and the actual experience of heavy users like himself. Third-party monitoring services that track GitHub's actual uptime appear to provide more accurate representations of the platform's real-world reliability from a user's perspective.

Implications and Context

The newsletter's headline poses an important question: "Why not other vendors?" This framing suggests the industry is asking why GitHub, owned by Microsoft and used by millions of developers, faces these reliability problems while competing services apparently do not experience comparable outages. The cascade of data integrity problems, security vulnerabilities, and availability issues raises questions about GitHub's infrastructure, engineering practices, and operational priorities during a period when it dominates the code repository market.

The combination of the data integrity incident affecting thousands of pull requests, the week-long series of outages disrupting core functionality, the security vulnerability affecting access controls, and the very public departure of a respected open-source leader creates a narrative of a critical service struggling with fundamental reliability and quality assurance challenges during what should be its period of maximum stability and maturity.

Date: Thu, 7 May 2026 14:24:49 +0000

Summary: Measuring Success for Meta Ads for Local Business

Lewis C. Lin's newsletter addresses a fundamental disconnect in how Meta Ads measures success for local businesses. The core problem is that Meta's standard advertising metrics—impressions, clicks, and engagement—are designed for digital marketers but fail to capture what actually matters to local business owners: real foot traffic and genuine return on investment.

The Core Problem

Meta Ads for Local Business promises to drive customers to physical neighborhood locations, yet the platform relies on metrics that have become disconnected from actual business outcomes. A restaurant owner doesn't care about how many people saw their ad or clicked on it if their tables remain empty on Saturday night. This fundamental mismatch between what Meta measures and what local business owners need represents a critical gap in the advertising ecosystem.

Key Stakeholders

The newsletter identifies two primary categories of stakeholders. The primary stakeholders are local businesses seeking customers and meaningful return on their advertising investments. These are restaurants, retail shops, service providers, and other neighborhood establishments that depend on foot traffic and local sales to survive. They measure success in tangible metrics: reservations booked, items sold, customers served, and revenue generated.

Secondary stakeholders are local consumers who discover relevant nearby businesses and services through Meta's advertising platform. For Meta's local advertising to work effectively, consumers need to see ads that are genuinely useful and relevant to their immediate geographic area and needs.

The Measurement Gap

Traditional digital advertising metrics fail local businesses for several reasons. Impressions indicate how many people saw an ad, but this tells a local restaurant nothing about whether those people are actually interested in dining there or if they even live nearby. Clicks show engagement with an ad, but clicking on a restaurant's advertisement doesn't guarantee a reservation or a visit. Engagement metrics measure likes, comments, and shares, but these social signals don't translate into the primary goal: customers walking through the door.

The newsletter emphasizes that Meta has built an impressive advertising infrastructure designed primarily for e-commerce, software companies, and other digital-first businesses that can directly measure online conversions. When a consumer clicks an ad for an online store and makes a purchase, that conversion is immediately trackable. However, local businesses operate in the physical world, and the journey from digital ad to actual store visit involves steps that are much harder to measure and attribute to the advertising campaign.

Why Standard Metrics Don't Work

Standard digital advertising metrics assume a direct online purchase funnel that doesn't exist for most local businesses. A person might see a Meta ad for a local coffee shop, not click on it, but then remember the shop the next day and visit. From Meta's perspective, this conversion would be invisible. Alternatively, someone might click on the ad but never visit, perhaps because they live too far away or the location doesn't fit their schedule.

Additionally, local businesses often have limited advertising budgets compared to large national brands. They need every advertising dollar to produce measurable results, making it critical that their investment translates into real foot traffic and sales. When they can only see impressions and clicks, they cannot confidently justify continued spending on Meta Ads.

The Need for Better Measurement

The newsletter argues for a new approach to measuring Meta Ads for Local Business success, one that focuses on outcome metrics rather than engagement metrics. Foot traffic represents the most direct measure of success for a local business. Has the advertising campaign actually brought more customers to the physical location? This can be measured through various methods: increased store visits, higher foot traffic on specific days when ads run, or customer surveys asking how they heard about the business.

Sales and revenue metrics are equally important. Did the advertising campaign result in more transactions and higher revenue? A restaurant owner might measure this through reservation increases, higher average customer counts during meal times, or increased cash register transactions on days corresponding to ad campaigns.

Customer acquisition and retention metrics matter as well. Are the ads bringing new customers who haven't visited before, or are they primarily reaching existing customers? Understanding customer acquisition cost from Meta advertising specifically helps local businesses determine whether their advertising spend is efficient.

Implementation Challenges

Creating better measurement systems for local advertising isn't simple. Meta would need to develop deeper integration with local business systems—point-of-sale terminals, reservation systems, and customer databases—to track whether ads actually drive store visits and sales. Privacy concerns would arise around tracking customer behavior from digital ad exposure to physical store visits. Additionally, many small local businesses lack sophisticated tracking systems, making it difficult to attribute sales to specific advertising campaigns.

Implications

The newsletter suggests that Meta faces an opportunity and a challenge. The opportunity lies in becoming the definitive local advertising platform by solving the measurement problem. If Meta can prove that its ads actually drive foot traffic and sales for local businesses at a favorable cost per customer acquired, local business owners would shift more advertising budget toward Meta.

The challenge is that solving this measurement problem requires moving beyond Meta's core competencies in digital advertising measurement. It requires understanding local business operations, integrating with diverse business systems, and potentially creating new measurement standards that the industry hasn't fully established.

Conclusion

Lewis C. Lin's newsletter articulates a critical issue in local digital advertising: the disconnect between what platforms measure and what local businesses actually need to know. Until Meta Ads for Local Business can demonstrate impact through foot traffic, sales, and customer acquisition metrics rather than impressions and clicks, local business owners will struggle to justify their advertising investments with confidence. The platform must evolve its measurement capabilities to reflect the real-world outcomes that determine success for neighborhood businesses.

Container Design Patterns for Distributed Systems
Date: Thu, 7 May 2026 15:31:08 +0000

Container Design Patterns for Distributed Systems

For much of their existence, containers have been viewed primarily as a deployment mechanism. The traditional understanding was straightforward: package your code together with its dependencies, create a single unit from this combination, and deploy it consistently across all environments. While this perspective remains valid and continues to provide significant utility, it represents only half of what containers are capable of achieving. The other half emerges when we shift our thinking from containers as vehicles for delivering individual applications to containers as fundamental building blocks that can be composed and combined with one another.

This conceptual evolution mirrors a similar transition that occurred earlier in software engineering history. During the 1990s, object-oriented programming introduced a clean architectural boundary for application code that could be composed against other code. This boundary enabled the development of design patterns, which became a standard toolkit of proven solutions that experienced programmers regularly draw upon. Distributed systems have undergone an analogous transformation with containers. Over the past decade, a collection of patterns has emerged and crystallized as engineers repeatedly encountered and solved the same architectural challenges.

The newsletter outlines six primary container design patterns organized by the scope of coordination they address. Three patterns apply to scenarios where containers share a single machine and need to cooperate closely. The remaining three patterns describe how containers coordinate work that extends across multiple machines in a distributed environment.

The article emphasizes an important conceptual point: none of these patterns should be considered absolute rules or mandatory requirements. Rather, they represent answers to recurring problems that have proven useful across many implementations by distributed-systems engineers. They are pragmatic solutions that have emerged through collective experience rather than theoretical prescriptions.

The patterns discussed are not new inventions but rather systematized knowledge about problems that practitioners continually encounter and resolve. This systematization is valuable because it allows engineers to recognize situations where established solutions already exist, reducing the need to design solutions from scratch each time a familiar problem arises.

The foundation of this discussion rests on understanding containers as composition units. When containers were first widely adopted, the focus was on their packaging and deployment capabilities. A container solved the problem of environment consistency by ensuring that code behaved the same way regardless of where it was deployed. This remains true and continues to provide value.

However, the more sophisticated application of containers involves recognizing that they can serve as standardized components that work together as part of larger systems. This compositional approach is particularly powerful in distributed systems, where applications span multiple machines and must coordinate their activities. By treating containers as composable units with well-defined interfaces and responsibilities, engineers can build complex systems from simpler, more manageable pieces.

The article references the pattern-driven approach that emerged from object-oriented programming as a parallel development. In object-oriented programming, design patterns became a shared language that allowed programmers to discuss solutions to common problems. Gang of Four patterns like Singleton, Factory, and Observer became widely recognized and taught to new programmers. Similarly, container design patterns now provide a shared vocabulary for discussing how distributed systems should be structured and how containers should interact.

The organization of the patterns by coordination scope is particularly instructive. The Sequence Opinion #856: Why Tomorrow's Dominant AI Agent Platforms Won't Be Today's Software Giants

The fundamental assumption underlying software development for decades has undergone a seismic shift with the emergence of AI agents. For the internet's first few decades, the entire software ecosystem operated from a single, unquestioned premise: the user was human. This human user possessed a predictable set of characteristics and behaviors that shaped every layer of the digital stack. They had eyes capable of reading interfaces, a browser for navigation, a mouse for interaction, a password manager for credentials, a credit card for transactions, an email address for communication, a tolerance for modal dialogs, and most importantly, a finite amount of patience that frustrated with slow loading times and complex workflows.

The entire architecture of Software-as-a-Service and consumer internet applications developed around this human user shape. Search engines like Google were engineered to rank web pages optimized for human consumption and human search behavior. E-commerce platforms like Amazon constructed elaborate conversion funnels specifically designed to guide human shoppers through decision-making processes. Customer relationship management systems such as Salesforce were built to track human sales representatives conducting human-to-human transactions with human buyers. Identity and authentication systems evolved to verify that actual humans were accessing accounts. Analytics platforms measured distinctly human activities: mouse clicks, browser sessions, purchase conversions.

This human-centric design paradigm proved so durable and successful that it became invisible. It was simply how software worked. Companies built moats and achieved dominance by optimizing their user experiences for this human shape, and the largest technology companies in the world emerged as the undisputed leaders by perfecting interfaces, platforms, and ecosystems around human users.

Then AI agents arrived, and this foundational assumption began to crack.

An AI agent does not have eyes that scan a visual interface. It does not navigate with a mouse or keyboard. It does not maintain a password manager filled with credentials, nor does it have a credit card or email address associated with a legal person. An AI agent has no patience threshold to exceed and no frustration with modal dialogs because it experiences none of these things. Most fundamentally, an AI agent does not interact with software the way humans do.

This distinction matters far more than it might initially appear, because it suggests something counterintuitive about the future of enterprise software: the companies that will dominate the AI agent era may not be the companies that dominated the human internet era.

The newsletter argues that current software giants achieved their dominance by optimizing for human users. Salesforce became the dominant CRM by building the best interface for human sales reps. Google became the search giant by ranking pages in ways that matched human search intent. Slack became the workplace communication standard by creating an experience optimized for human communication patterns. Microsoft built Office into an inescapable monopoly by making tools that human knowledge workers could not imagine working without.

But AI agents require fundamentally different infrastructure. An agent needs APIs rather than user interfaces. It needs programmatic access rather than visual dashboards. It needs standardized data formats and reliable, consistent machine-readable outputs rather than displays optimized for human eyes. An agent needs permission systems that grant capabilities rather than passwords that grant access. An agent needs audit trails that track what happened rather than analytics that track how humans behaved.

This creates what might be called the "agent infrastructure gap." The software platforms that dominated the human era were optimized for a set of constraints and requirements that AI agents do not share. Their success in the human era may actually become a disadvantage in the agent era, because their entire architecture, product philosophy, and business model assumes a human user at the center of every interaction.

Consider Salesforce. It became the market leader in CRMs by building beautiful interfaces that human sales reps could use intuitively. Sales reps log in, update records, run reports, manage pipelines. The entire system is designed around this human workflow. But what happens when an AI agent needs to access Salesforce to perform sales activities on behalf of a company? The agent does not need the beautiful interface. It needs clean APIs, predictable response formats, granular permission controls, transparent pricing per API call, and infrastructure designed for thousands of concurrent requests from non-human users.

The same pattern applies to Google. Google achieved dominance by understanding how humans search and ranking results accordingly. But an AI agent does not "search" the way humans do. It does not type queries into a search box and click through results. It needs structured data access, bulk retrieval capabilities, and machine-readable information extraction at scale.

This suggests that the next generation of dominant software platforms will be built by companies willing to architect their entire stack around non-human users from the ground up. These companies will make different trade-offs. They will optimize for API performance over user interface beauty. They will prioritize machine-readable data formats over human-readable dashboards. They will build permission systems designed for autonomous agents rather than authenticated humans. They will price based on capability and throughput rather than seat licenses.

The companies that will become the "Salesforce of agents" or the "Google of agents" will likely not be Salesforce or Google. Instead, they will be companies that understand the fundamentally different requirements of agent-centric software and build platforms from first principles to serve those requirements. These could be entirely new companies founded by entrepreneurs who recognize this shift, or they could be existing companies that are willing to fundamentally reimagine their platforms around agent infrastructure rather than human interfaces.
